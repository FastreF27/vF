Chapter Two · The Adaptive Discipline

Stay loose.

Every real meeting goes off-script in the first five minutes. This chapter is about the two skills that matter most once it does: reading the room you are actually in, and interviewing like a conversation rather than a performance.

01.
Room Reading

Read the room before you read your notes.

The audience is not a distraction from the speech. The audience is the speech.

The Skill

Reading the room means adjusting — in real time — to what you are seeing and hearing from the people in front of you. Their energy, their attention, the questions they are not asking. A great speaker treats the audience as a second instrument, not a passive receiver. If the room is warm, you can slow down and land a joke. If the room is cold, you had better get to the point. If someone has their camera off and is barely engaged, you change tack entirely.

"The interviewer had his camera off, barely knew my name, and had probably never glanced at my resume. I felt utterly numb. I had no idea how to warm up." — opening of my persuasive speech, and the exact moment I realized this skill is the whole game

The Evidence

The interview that made me write a speech about it
Co-op recruiting · late 2025

I hit every technical. I had the answers cold. I walked out feeling like I had failed — because I had never learned how to handle a human who refused to meet me halfway. That interview became the opening anecdote of my persuasive speech, but more importantly, it reset how I prepare for every conversation now.

I no longer prepare answers. I prepare responses to energy. What do I do if the interviewer is warm? What do I do if they are cold? What do I do if they interrupt? What do I do if they go off-topic? The answers are different, and none of them come from my resume.

How It Shows Up at Work

In my Peak Technology Partners co-op, no two diligence calls feel the same. Some clients want you to cut to the numbers. Others want to tell you the history of the company for twenty minutes first. The analyst who forces the same agenda onto every call loses the room every time. The one who reads the CEO's energy in the first ninety seconds and matches it — patient with some, crisp with others — gets meaningful information out of the call.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Look at faces, not at your notes. Your notes do not change. The room does. Make real eye contact with one person for a sentence, then move. You will start feeling where attention is dropping before you consciously notice it.
  2. Build in checkpoints where you can pivot. Structure your talk so that after each major section, you can either go deeper or move on, depending on what the room is doing. Having the option is the whole point.
  3. Get comfortable with silence. A two-second pause after a point feels like forever to the speaker and like nothing to the audience. Silence gives you time to read the room. Filling it with "um" gives the audience time to disengage.
02.
Interviewing

An interview is a conversation that remembers it is one.

The resume got you in the room. Nothing else it does matters once you are there.

The Skill

Interviewing well is the applied form of everything in this portfolio: a story (yours), prepared anchors (the three or four experiences you absolutely need to surface), a read on the room (is this person in the mood to talk or to be sold to?), and a pitch (you). I used to prepare for interviews by over-studying the company. Now I prepare by rehearsing the handful of stories about myself that can be redeployed for almost any question I might get.

The Evidence

Peak Technology Partners — Investment Banking Analyst Co-op
Landed · active through Spring 2026

Going in, I had four stories locked and loaded: a moment I had to learn something hard on the fly, a moment I had to handle a difficult team dynamic, a deal I had followed closely and could talk about in detail, and a specific reason I wanted this seat and not a generic IB seat. Almost every question I got mapped onto one of those four stories. I was not improvising content. I was improvising delivery.

The interviewer also asked me something I had not planned for — a curveball about my biochemistry background and why I had shifted to finance. Because I was not locked into a script, I could answer honestly: the work I most enjoyed in STEM was the part where I had to explain complicated things to people who did not share my technical context. That is finance client work. That was not a rehearsed line. It was the truth, and it worked because I was not too locked in to say it.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every networking coffee, every co-op touchpoint, every performance conversation — they are all interviews wearing different outfits. The people who thrive in finance are not the ones with the best scripts. They are the ones who can walk into any room, figure out what the other person needs from the next thirty minutes, and deliver it without losing their own voice.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Pre-stage 4–5 stories, not answers to 50 questions. Most behavioral questions are the same three questions in different hats. Build a small library of stories — each with a clear situation, action, and result — that you can re-pattern for whatever the interviewer asks. You'll stop sounding rehearsed and start sounding like someone who knows themselves.
  2. Ask one real question, not five polite ones. End-of-interview questions are a reading test. "What's one thing your best analyst did in their first six months that surprised you?" is a real question. "Tell me about the culture" is a filler question. Real questions get real answers — and they signal you are actually listening.
  3. Debrief every interview the same day. Write down what landed, what didn't, what you wish you'd said. Do this even when the interview went well — especially when it went well. Most of what I know about interviewing came from the post-mortems, not the interviews themselves.
