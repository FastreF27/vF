Chapter One · The Story Discipline

Tell a story.

A story is a scaffold the audience will carry for you. Points are weight they have to lift. This chapter covers how I build narrative into every speaking moment — and how I prepare for it without turning it into a script.

01.
Narrative Speaking

Lead with the story, not the slide deck.

Data proves a point. A story makes someone care about the point in the first place.

The Skill

Narrative speaking means organizing everything — a pitch, a class presentation, an answer in an interview — around a sequence the listener can follow without effort: a situation, a tension, a turn, a resolution. Instead of opening with a definition or a statistic, you open with a scene. The data shows up later, and when it does, it lands harder because the audience already cares about the story it is inside.

"When I walk into a presentation, I ask myself: if I gave this speech with no slides, no notes, and no podium — would it still work? If the answer is no, I am not giving a speech, I am reading a document out loud." — from the draft notes, COMM 1113

The Evidence

Informative Speech — The Rosenhan Experiment
Feb 2026 · 6 min · Dr. Nora Suren

I could have opened the speech with a definition of psychiatric diagnosis. Instead, I opened with an Instagram reel of a street interviewer asking psychiatrists a simple question: how many patients have you actually cured? None of them had a clean answer. That was the hook. From there, the audience was already inside the question — and Rosenhan's 1973 study became the payoff, not the premise.

The real takeaway wasn't about psychiatry at all. It was about reputation: once a label lands on you, everything you do gets read through it. I did not have to argue that point. The story argued it for me.

How It Shows Up at Work

In investment banking, every pitch deck is ostensibly a story — situation, problem, solution, ask — but most of them read like a data dump with a cover page. The analysts whose decks actually get remembered are the ones who can, in a single sentence, tell me why this deal matters and why now. That is narrative framing doing the heavy lifting before a single multiple gets defended.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Write the one-sentence version first. Before you build any slide or outline, answer this: "If I could only tell them one sentence, what would it be?" That sentence is your spine. Every other piece of content in the talk either serves it or gets cut.
  2. Open inside the story, not before it. Skip the throat-clearing. Skip the "today I am going to talk about…" Put the audience in a scene — an image, a question, a moment — in the first fifteen seconds. Context can follow. Attention cannot be recovered.
  3. Put the data where the story needs it. Statistics are ammunition, not narrative. Hold them until a specific moment in the story requires them — then fire. A single statistic inside a story hits harder than ten in a row on a slide.
02.
Prepared, Not Scripted

Prepare anchors, not paragraphs.

The goal of preparation is not to remove improvisation. It is to make improvisation safe.

The Skill

There is a difference between walking into a speaking moment prepared and walking in scripted. Prepared means you have rehearsed the structure, you know the three or four points you absolutely have to hit, you have memorized a single sharp quote or statistic, and you know how you want to open and close. Scripted means you have memorized paragraphs — and the moment you drop one, the whole thing collapses.

I have learned to prepare in anchors: a handful of short phrases, one or two numbers, an opening image, a closing line. Everything between the anchors is live. It has to be — because that is where the audience actually lives.

Scripts lock you into a thought. Anchors let your mind run free.

The Evidence

Persuasive Speech — "We've Been Going to School for the Wrong Skills"
Spring 2026 · 7 min · Monroe Motivated Sequence

The outline for this speech ran about four pages. The version I actually gave fit on roughly ten index cards worth of phrases. I memorized the McKinsey statistic (14% growth in demand for social and emotional skills by 2030) and the Center for Security and Emerging Technology finding (58% of in-demand skills in growing jobs are non-technical). Everything else I spoke in real time.

When a classmate pushed back mid-talk about whether AI really could replace technical work, I did not have to find my place in a script — I had room to engage with the challenge and roll it into my next point. That flexibility is not a nice-to-have in professional speaking. It is the job.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every real meeting — client call, interview, pitch — goes off-script in the first five minutes. Somebody asks a question you did not plan for. Somebody is late. Somebody pushes on an assumption. If you wrote paragraphs, you are now editing paragraphs in your head while you are supposed to be listening. If you wrote anchors, you are just having a conversation.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Memorize the opening, the closing, and three anchor lines. Nothing else. The opening because the first thirty seconds set the temperature of the room. The closing because people remember it. The three middle anchors because they are the ideas you came to deliver.
  2. Practice out loud, not in your head. Silent rehearsal tricks you into thinking you are ready. Speaking it out loud — ideally to a wall, a friend, a voice recorder — surfaces the sentences that will not survive live delivery.
  3. Rehearse getting interrupted. Have someone throw a question at you mid-talk. If you can fold the interruption into your next anchor smoothly, you are prepared. If it derails you, your prep is still operating like a script.
