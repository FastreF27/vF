Thesis · In Five Words

Tell a story. Stay loose.

Good professional speaking is not a monologue you perform. It is a conversation you steer. Every real-world speaking situation I have been in this semester — interviews, pitches, classroom debates, co-op meetings — has rewarded the same three things: a narrative the audience can follow, a small set of anchor points I actually know cold, and enough room to move when the room changes on me.

What follows is the rest of the sentence: five skills, a pile of evidence, and what I plan to do with all of it.

II.
Five Skills · The Working Kit
The core of the portfolio

The five things
I actually use.

There is a version of this project where I list every technique from the textbook, define it dutifully, and hand in something that sounds exactly like everybody else's. This is not that version.

These are the five skills I reached for this semester when the stakes were real — in a co-op interview, in front of a finance class, across the table from a hiring partner. Each one comes with evidence. Each one I am still working on.

01.
Narrative Speaking

Lead with the story, not the slide deck.

A story is a scaffold the audience will carry for you. Points are weight they have to lift.

The Skill

Narrative speaking means organizing everything — a pitch, a class presentation, an answer in an interview — around a sequence the listener can follow without effort: a situation, a tension, a turn, a resolution. Instead of opening with a definition or a statistic, you open with a scene. The data shows up later, and when it does, it lands harder because the audience already cares about the story it is inside.

"When I walk into a presentation, I ask myself: if I gave this speech with no slides, no notes, and no podium — would it still work? If the answer is no, I am not giving a speech, I am reading a document out loud." — from the draft notes, COMM 1113

The Evidence

Informative Speech — The Rosenhan Experiment
Feb 2026 · 6 min · Dr. Nora Suren

I could have opened the speech with a definition of psychiatric diagnosis. Instead, I opened with an Instagram reel of a street interviewer asking psychiatrists a simple question: how many patients have you actually cured? None of them had a clean answer. That was the hook. From there, the audience was already inside the question — and Rosenhan's 1973 study became the payoff, not the premise.

The real takeaway wasn't about psychiatry at all. It was about reputation: once a label lands on you, everything you do gets read through it. I did not have to argue that point. The story argued it for me.

How It Shows Up at Work

In investment banking, every pitch deck is ostensibly a story — situation, problem, solution, ask — but most of them read like a data dump with a cover page. The analysts whose decks actually get remembered are the ones who can, in a single sentence, tell me why this deal matters and why now. That is narrative framing doing the heavy lifting before a single multiple gets defended.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Write the one-sentence version first. Before you build any slide or outline, answer this: "If I could only tell them one sentence, what would it be?" That sentence is your spine. Every other piece of content in the talk either serves it or gets cut.
  2. Open inside the story, not before it. Skip the throat-clearing. Skip the "today I am going to talk about…" Put the audience in a scene — an image, a question, a moment — in the first fifteen seconds. Context can follow. Attention cannot be recovered.
  3. Put the data where the story needs it. Statistics are ammunition, not narrative. Hold them until a specific moment in the story requires them — then fire. A single statistic inside a story hits harder than ten in a row on a slide.
02.
Prepared, Not Scripted

Prepare anchors, not paragraphs.

The goal of preparation is not to remove improvisation. It is to make improvisation safe.

The Skill

There is a difference between walking into a speaking moment prepared and walking in scripted. Prepared means you have rehearsed the structure, you know the three or four points you absolutely have to hit, you have memorized a single sharp quote or statistic, and you know how you want to open and close. Scripted means you have memorized paragraphs — and the moment you drop one, the whole thing collapses.

I have learned to prepare in anchors: a handful of short phrases, one or two numbers, an opening image, a closing line. Everything between the anchors is live. It has to be — because that is where the audience actually lives.

Scripts lock you into a thought. Anchors let your mind run free.

The Evidence

Persuasive Speech — "We've Been Going to School for the Wrong Skills"
Spring 2026 · 7 min · Monroe Motivated Sequence

The outline for this speech ran about four pages. The version I actually gave fit on roughly ten index cards worth of phrases. I memorized the McKinsey statistic (14% growth in demand for social and emotional skills by 2030) and the Center for Security and Emerging Technology finding (58% of in-demand skills in growing jobs are non-technical). Everything else I spoke in real time.

When a classmate pushed back mid-talk about whether AI really could replace technical work, I did not have to find my place in a script — I had room to engage with the challenge and roll it into my next point. That flexibility is not a nice-to-have in professional speaking. It is the job.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every real meeting — client call, interview, pitch — goes off-script in the first five minutes. Somebody asks a question you did not plan for. Somebody is late. Somebody pushes on an assumption. If you wrote paragraphs, you are now editing paragraphs in your head while you are supposed to be listening. If you wrote anchors, you are just having a conversation.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Memorize the opening, the closing, and three anchor lines. Nothing else. The opening because the first thirty seconds set the temperature of the room. The closing because people remember it. The three middle anchors because they are the ideas you came to deliver.
  2. Practice out loud, not in your head. Silent rehearsal tricks you into thinking you are ready. Speaking it out loud — ideally to a wall, a friend, a voice recorder — surfaces the sentences that will not survive live delivery.
  3. Rehearse getting interrupted. Have someone throw a question at you mid-talk. If you can fold the interruption into your next anchor smoothly, you are prepared. If it derails you, your prep is still operating like a script.
03.
Room Reading

Read the room before you read your notes.

The audience is not a distraction from the speech. The audience is the speech.

The Skill

Reading the room means adjusting — in real time — to what you are seeing and hearing from the people in front of you. Their energy, their attention, the questions they are not asking. A great speaker treats the audience as a second instrument, not a passive receiver. If the room is warm, you can slow down and land a joke. If the room is cold, you had better get to the point. If someone has their camera off and is barely engaged, you change tack entirely.

"The interviewer had his camera off, barely knew my name, and had probably never glanced at my resume. I felt utterly numb. I had no idea how to warm up." — opening of my persuasive speech, and the exact moment I realized this skill is the whole game

The Evidence

The interview that made me write a speech about it
Co-op recruiting · late 2025

I hit every technical. I had the answers cold. I walked out feeling like I had failed — because I had never learned how to handle a human who refused to meet me halfway. That interview became the opening anecdote of my persuasive speech, but more importantly, it reset how I prepare for every conversation now.

I no longer prepare answers. I prepare responses to energy. What do I do if the interviewer is warm? What do I do if they are cold? What do I do if they interrupt? What do I do if they go off-topic? The answers are different, and none of them come from my resume.

How It Shows Up at Work

In my Peak Technology Partners co-op, no two diligence calls feel the same. Some clients want you to cut to the numbers. Others want to tell you the history of the company for twenty minutes first. The analyst who forces the same agenda onto every call loses the room every time. The one who reads the CEO's energy in the first ninety seconds and matches it — patient with some, crisp with others — gets meaningful information out of the call.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Look at faces, not at your notes. Your notes do not change. The room does. Make real eye contact with one person for a sentence, then move. You will start feeling where attention is dropping before you consciously notice it.
  2. Build in checkpoints where you can pivot. Structure your talk so that after each major section, you can either go deeper or move on, depending on what the room is doing. Having the option is the whole point.
  3. Get comfortable with silence. A two-second pause after a point feels like forever to the speaker and like nothing to the audience. Silence gives you time to read the room. Filling it with "um" gives the audience time to disengage.
04.
Pitching & Persuasion

A great pitch sells the feeling, not the spreadsheet.

People fund stories. The numbers only exist to defend the story once they've already decided they like it.

The Skill

Pitching is its own animal. It is half persuasive speech, half sales call, half research presentation, and entirely about earning a specific decision from a specific person in a specific amount of time. The best pitches I have studied — Aperol, actual Shark Tank episodes, the decks that circulate inside IB — all do the same thing: they open with a feeling, they land a sharp "why now," they prove the market, and they close with a specific ask.

The Evidence

Shark Tank Assignment — Cello's Limoncello
Feb 2026 · Live pitch · Built with my friend Marcello

We opened with a scene: you are at dinner, you want a limoncello, the server shrugs and says they don't carry it. That is the whole problem in one image. 7,000+ Italian restaurants in the U.S. and no dominant limoncello brand. Then we ran the Aperol playbook — a $1.3B brand built on one cocktail — and made the ask: $200,000 for 15% equity. Closing line: Life's short. Make it a Cello.

We got laughs. We also got real engagement on the numbers. That was the whole point — a pitch can be fun and rigorous. Those are not opposites. The fun is what earned us the room's attention long enough to defend the rigor.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every banker pitch is a persuasive speech with a deck attached. The deck is the evidence. The speaker is the story. I have sat in on enough of them now to see the pattern: the MD who tells a clean narrative and lets the associate defend the model on follow-ups gets the mandate. The one who reads the deck out loud does not.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Write the tagline before you write the deck. If you cannot summarize the pitch in one line that a friend at a bar would remember, the deck will not save you. "Life's short. Make it a Cello." took a day to land. The rest of the deck took a week.
  2. Lead with the problem, not the product. Your product is boring to them. Their problem is not. Spend the first 60 seconds making them feel the gap your product fills — then reveal the product as the answer to the tension you built.
  3. Have a specific ask. Vague asks ("let's keep talking") get vague replies. Specific asks ("$200K for 15% equity, decision by Q3") either close the deal or surface the real objection — both of which are better than drifting.
05.
Interviewing

An interview is a conversation that remembers it is one.

The resume got you in the room. Nothing else it does matters once you are there.

The Skill

Interviewing well is the applied form of everything above: a story (yours), prepared anchors (the three or four experiences you absolutely need to surface), a read on the room (is this person in the mood to talk or to be sold to?), and a pitch (you). I used to prepare for interviews by over-studying the company. Now I prepare by rehearsing the handful of stories about myself that can be redeployed for almost any question I might get.

The Evidence

Peak Technology Partners — Investment Banking Analyst Co-op
Landed · active through Spring 2026

Going in, I had four stories locked and loaded: a moment I had to learn something hard on the fly, a moment I had to handle a difficult team dynamic, a deal I had followed closely and could talk about in detail, and a specific reason I wanted this seat and not a generic IB seat. Almost every question I got mapped onto one of those four stories. I was not improvising content. I was improvising delivery.

The interviewer also asked me something I had not planned for — a curveball about my biochemistry background and why I had shifted to finance. Because I was not locked into a script, I could answer honestly: the work I most enjoyed in STEM was the part where I had to explain complicated things to people who did not share my technical context. That is finance client work. That was not a rehearsed line. It was the truth, and it worked because I was not too locked in to say it.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every networking coffee, every co-op touchpoint, every performance conversation — they are all interviews wearing different outfits. The people who thrive in finance are not the ones with the best scripts. They are the ones who can walk into any room, figure out what the other person needs from the next thirty minutes, and deliver it without losing their own voice.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Pre-stage 4–5 stories, not answers to 50 questions. Most behavioral questions are the same three questions in different hats. Build a small library of stories — each with a clear situation, action, and result — that you can re-pattern for whatever the interviewer asks. You'll stop sounding rehearsed and start sounding like someone who knows themselves.
  2. Ask one real question, not five polite ones. End-of-interview questions are a reading test. "What's one thing your best analyst did in their first six months that surprised you?" is a real question. "Tell me about the culture" is a filler question. Real questions get real answers — and they signal you are actually listening.
  3. Debrief every interview the same day. Write down what landed, what didn't, what you wish you'd said. Do this even when the interview went well — especially when it went well. Most of what I know about interviewing came from the post-mortems, not the interviews themselves.
III.
The Takeaway
Final
One key takeaway for the future

Stop rehearsing. Start listening.

If I had to collapse this entire semester into a single discipline I will carry into every future meeting, pitch, and interview, it is this: the best thing I can do in the thirty seconds before I open my mouth is stop practicing what I am about to say and start listening to what the room is actually doing.

That is it. That is the upgrade. I used to think great speaking was about the speaker — their command, their polish, their poise. I don't believe that anymore. Great speaking is about the speaker's relationship to the audience in a specific moment. Everything else — the narrative, the anchors, the prep, the pitch, the interview technique — exists to free you up to be fully present in that relationship when the moment finally arrives.

My professional life is going to involve a lot of rooms. A lot of interviews, a lot of pitches, a lot of difficult conversations where I need to land the right thing at the right time. The script cannot save me in those rooms. The story can. And so can paying attention.
