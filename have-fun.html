Chapter Three · The Enjoyment Principle

Have fun doing it.

Business and professional speaking is often framed as stuffy, dense, and boring. I don't buy it. The best professional communicators I have watched — in pitches, in interviews, in classrooms — keep a sense of play without ever sacrificing rigor. This chapter is about how they do it, told through the pitch I am most proud of.

Featured Case Study

Cello's Limoncello.

A Shark Tank-style pitch I built with my friend Marcello for an entirely real product concept: a premium American limoncello brand aimed at a $4.5B category with almost no dominant player. The pitch got laughs. It also got a serious conversation about unit economics. That is the point.

Pitching & Persuasion

A great pitch sells the feeling, not the spreadsheet.

People fund stories. The numbers only exist to defend the story once they've already decided they like it.

The Skill

Pitching is its own animal. It is half persuasive speech, half sales call, half research presentation, and entirely about earning a specific decision from a specific person in a specific amount of time. The best pitches I have studied — Aperol, actual Shark Tank episodes, the decks that circulate inside IB — all do the same thing: they open with a feeling, they land a sharp "why now," they prove the market, and they close with a specific ask.

But here is the thing nobody tells you: the best ones are also fun. Not gimmicky-fun. Not cringe-fun. But there is a lightness in the delivery — a confidence that lets the speaker enjoy themselves, crack a line, let the audience laugh — without undermining the case underneath. That lightness is not a distraction from the rigor. It is what earns the rigor a hearing.

Fun and rigor are not opposites. The fun is what earns you the room's attention long enough to defend the rigor.

The Evidence

Shark Tank Assignment — Cello's Limoncello
Feb 2026 · Live pitch · Built with my friend Marcello

We opened with a scene: you are at dinner, you want a limoncello, the server shrugs and says they don't carry it. That is the whole problem in one image. 7,000+ Italian restaurants in the U.S. and no dominant limoncello brand. Then we ran the Aperol playbook — a $1.3B brand built on one cocktail — and made the ask: $200,000 for 15% equity. Closing line: Life's short. Make it a Cello.

We got laughs. We also got real engagement on the numbers. Margins (3.3× on-premise, 4.1× retail). Go-to-market (50 Italian restaurants in Boston and NYC, then specialty retail, then distribution). Why now (low-ABV boom, dolce vita trending, no incumbent). Every joke was a scaffold for a real data point. Every data point was resting on a joke that let the room lean in.

How It Shows Up at Work

Every banker pitch is a persuasive speech with a deck attached. The deck is the evidence. The speaker is the story. I have sat in on enough of them now to see the pattern: the MD who tells a clean narrative — with personality — and lets the associate defend the model on follow-ups gets the mandate. The one who reads the deck out loud does not. Personality is not a liability in professional settings. It is what makes the rest of the work memorable.

How to Get Better at It

  1. Write the tagline before you write the deck. If you cannot summarize the pitch in one line that a friend at a bar would remember, the deck will not save you. "Life's short. Make it a Cello." took a day to land. The rest of the deck took a week.
  2. Lead with the problem, not the product. Your product is boring to them. Their problem is not. Spend the first 60 seconds making them feel the gap your product fills — then reveal the product as the answer to the tension you built.
  3. Have a specific ask. Vague asks ("let's keep talking") get vague replies. Specific asks ("$200K for 15% equity, decision by Q3") either close the deal or surface the real objection — both of which are better than drifting.
  4. Give yourself permission to be a person. The audience is not grading your professionalism. They are deciding whether they want to be in a room with you for the next twenty years. Let them see that you are interested in the work, enjoying the moment, and actually having a good time. It will disarm people who walked in ready to tear the idea apart.
